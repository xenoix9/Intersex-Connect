Common Medical Questions

Answers to frequently asked questions about intersex medical conditions and treatments.

This resource provides answers to common questions about intersex variations, medical care, and treatments. The information is intended for educational purposes and should not replace consultation with qualified healthcare providers.

Understanding Intersex Variations

What does "intersex" mean? +

Intersex is an umbrella term for people born with physical sex characteristics that don't fit typical binary notions of male or female bodies. These variations can involve chromosomes, gonads, hormones, or genitals. Intersex is about biological variations, not gender identity.

How common are intersex variations? +

Intersex variations are more common than many people realize. Estimates suggest that approximately 1.7% of the population is born with intersex traits - that's about the same percentage as people with red hair. Some variations are visible at birth, while others may not become apparent until puberty or later in life.

What are some examples of intersex variations? +

There are many different intersex variations, including:

  • Congenital Adrenal Hyperplasia (CAH)
  • Androgen Insensitivity Syndrome (AIS)
  • Klinefelter Syndrome (XXY)
  • Turner Syndrome (XO)
  • 5-alpha-reductase deficiency
  • Ovotesticular Differences of Sex Development

Each variation has unique characteristics and may require different approaches to medical care.

Medical Care & Treatments

Do all intersex variations require medical treatment? +

No, not all intersex variations require medical intervention. Many intersex people are healthy and don't need any medical treatment. Some variations may require monitoring or treatment for associated health conditions, but being intersex itself is not a medical problem that needs fixing. The focus should be on health and well-being, not on "normalizing" appearance.

What are the concerns about early surgeries on intersex children? +

Many intersex advocates and human rights organizations oppose non-consensual, medically unnecessary surgeries on intersex children. Concerns include:

  • Irreversible procedures performed before the child can consent
  • Risk of reduced or lost sexual sensation
  • Potential for incorrect gender assignment
  • Psychological trauma from repeated medical examinations and procedures
  • Violation of bodily autonomy and human rights

Many advocate for delaying non-essential procedures until the individual can participate in decision-making.

What is the standard of care for intersex children today? +

Medical guidelines are evolving. Many professional organizations now recommend:

  • Deferring non-essential surgeries until the child can participate in decision-making
  • Comprehensive, interdisciplinary care teams including mental health professionals
  • Full disclosure and transparency with patients and families
  • Respect for patient autonomy and bodily integrity
  • Support for the whole family, including connection with peer support groups

However, practices vary widely, and non-consensual surgeries still occur in many places.

What healthcare specialists might an intersex person see? +

Depending on their specific variation and needs, an intersex person might work with:

  • Endocrinologists (hormone specialists)
  • Urologists or gynecologists
  • Genetic counselors
  • Mental health professionals
  • Primary care physicians
  • Reproductive specialists

It's important to find providers who respect bodily autonomy and have experience with intersex variations.

Health & Development

Can intersex people have children? +

Fertility varies depending on the specific intersex variation. Some intersex people are fertile, while others may have reduced fertility or be infertile. Advances in reproductive technology have created more options for some intersex people who wish to have biological children. It's important to discuss fertility concerns with knowledgeable healthcare providers.

Do intersex people go through puberty normally? +

Puberty experiences vary widely among intersex people. Some go through typical puberty for their assigned gender, while others may experience:

  • Delayed puberty
  • Puberty that doesn't match their assigned gender
  • Need for hormone therapy to induce puberty
  • Mixed pubertal development (some characteristics of both typical male and female puberty)

Support from knowledgeable healthcare providers during this time is important.

Are there specific health risks associated with intersex variations? +

Some intersex variations are associated with specific health considerations that may need monitoring, such as:

  • Hormone imbalances that require management
  • Increased risk for certain types of tumors in specific variations
  • Bone density concerns in some variations
  • Cardiovascular issues associated with certain chromosomal variations

Regular check-ups with knowledgeable providers can help manage any health risks.

Psychosocial Aspects

How can parents support an intersex child? +

Parents can support their intersex child by:

  • Seeking information from reliable sources and intersex-led organizations
  • Connecting with other families with intersex children
  • Respecting their child's privacy and bodily autonomy
  • Delaying non-essential medical procedures until the child can participate in decisions
  • Using accurate, affirming language when discussing their child's body
  • Loving and accepting their child for who they are
What mental health support is available for intersex people? +

Mental health support is an important part of care for many intersex people and their families. This may include:

  • Therapy with providers knowledgeable about intersex issues
  • Peer support groups and community connections
  • Family counseling to help navigate medical decisions and disclosure
  • Support for dealing with stigma, discrimination, or medical trauma

Many intersex people find connection with the intersex community particularly valuable.

When and how should parents talk to their child about being intersex? +

Open, honest communication from an early age is generally recommended. Approaches include:

  • Using age-appropriate language
  • Normalizing bodily diversity
  • Answering questions honestly as they arise
  • Providing more detailed information as the child grows older
  • Emphasizing that being intersex is a natural variation, not something to be ashamed of

Many families find it helpful to work with counselors or connect with other families who have navigated these conversations.
